What Makes a Game Fun to Play

October 2005 · Mike Austin

This article presents a brief exploration into what makes a video game fun to play. It has been written to help me work out game ideas, with the intent that it may be useful to others. It concentrates mostly on video games, but other types are mentioned for comparison.

Interactivity

First and foremost, a game must be interactive, or else it would not be a game. Graphic 'demos' and screensavers are fun in their own right, but interactivity allows a person to be part of the word that is represented. Interactivity can be anything from answering yes/no questions, to virtual worlds where everything interacts with everything else.

Game Aspects

I see four weighted categories which define the aspect of a game:

Coordination How well can you react and respond to changing events
  This could be simply moving the mouse over a specific square, to flying a jet at mach 3. Little coordination is required for board games and puzzles.
Knowledge How well do you know the rules of the game and how much do you know about the the game details
  In trivia games, knowledge is king. For FPS games, knowing the map and the locations of weapons and vehicles is crucial. For chess, it's not only knowing what piece does what, but also all the possible strategic combinations of moves.
Capacity How many things can you keep in your head at once, or, dynamic knowledge
  While playing chess, how many moves ahead can you think. In a tactical war game, how many things can you remember such as where your opponent is or how many troops you have left.
Intuition How good are you at solving new problems and choosing the right decisions
  In chess, when should you sacrifice one of your own pieces. In an FPS game, where is the most likely place your opponent is hiding?

 

Attention to Detail

They say the devil is in the details, and it's true. Nothing is more boring than a screen that does nothing unless you do something, or a chess board that looks like an ordinary chess board. The accumulation of details is greater than the sum of its parts. Details are things that are not neccessary, but give the game a polished look or play.

Examples of details:

  • A multitude of special moves in a fighting game allow the player to keep learning new tactics.
  • When Sonic the Hedgehog is left idle, he will tap his foot impatiently.
  • In Battlefield 2, loud explosions blur your vision and muffle your hearing.
  • In most newer FPS games, as you run, your gun sways left and right

Skill Level

If you allow the player to set the skill level of a game, he or she can play the game at their own pace. Novices won't be put off by the game being to hard, and experts won't be put off by the game being too easy. As a bonus, a person can replay a game at a higher difficulty level when they have mastered the previous skill level, and it feels like a brand new game. In any event, people like to play with the chance of winning. If you feel chalenged and feel like you're winning, then everything is good.

What should the differences be between skill levels? For starters, a higher skill level may include more opponents, smarter opponents, different types of opponents, a faster paced play, a shorter time limit, and/or environmental or rule changes.

Feature Unlocking

Unlocking new features at certain scores or at experience levels entices the player to keep playing. They are rewarded.

Examples of locked features:

  • New weapons, vehicles, moves or abilities
  • New or enhanced levels

Rules of Play

To make a game enjoyable over months and months of play, there should be

cooperative teams, everyone for themselves, capture the flag, and variations of these.

Gameplay Options

Allowing the player or players modify game options is a simple way to add value to the game. Some FPS games allow you to modify the force of gravity, the spawn time between deaths, the effects of friendly fire, and so on.
